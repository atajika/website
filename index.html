About

Tracing life through
stone and time

I'm a paleontologist with a deep fascination for cephalopods — the group that gave us ammonoids, nautilids, and today's octopus and squid — and the clues they leave behind in ancient rock.

My research uses 3D morphometrics, computed tomography, and stable isotope geochemistry to reconstruct the ecology, growth, and metabolic rates of extinct and extant shell-bearing cephalopods. A central question driving my work is: why did ammonoids go extinct at the end of the Cretaceous while nautilids survived?

I completed my Ph.D. at the University of Zurich, then held JSPS postdoctoral fellowships at the American Museum of Natural History and the University of Tokyo. I am now based at Kyoto University as a Hakubi Research Fellow.

I believe science should be transparent, reproducible, and shared widely — including beyond specialist audiences.

Research Focus

What I study

Three interconnected questions about cephalopods, extinction, and deep-time ecology.

🐚

Ammonoid & Nautiloid Evolution

Using 3D morphometrics and CT scanning to document intraspecific variation, ontogenetic growth patterns, and the evolution of conch shape across geological time.

3D morphometricsCT scanningontogenytaxonomy
⚗️

Metabolic Rates & Selective Extinction

Investigating why ammonoids went extinct at the K-Pg boundary while nautilids survived, using stable carbon isotopes as a proxy for metabolic rate in fossil shell material.

stable isotopesK-Pg extinctionmetabolic proxies
🌊

Ecology of Living & Fossil Cephalopods

Reconstructing the habitat, reproductive biology, and ecology of cephalopods by combining isotopic analysis of modern Nautilus with the fossil record.
